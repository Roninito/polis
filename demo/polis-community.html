Our Origin

Three years ago, this block
had no organization.

The corner of Meridian and 5th had been a vacant lot for eleven years. The park benches were broken. The streetlights on the east side of Maple went out in 2019 and nobody fixed them. Residents had filed requests. Attended city council meetings. Written emails that went unanswered.

Diane Okafor, a retired urban planner who moved to the district in 2021, had a different idea. Instead of asking the city to govern the neighborhood, she proposed the neighborhood govern itself — raise its own money, vote on its own priorities, and execute its own projects.

"We stopped asking for permission. We wrote a charter, opened a treasury, and voted on what to fix first. The city caught up eventually."
— Diane Okafor, Founding Council Chair · Block Captain, Maple Street North

The POLIS Collective launched on January 1, 2022 with 31 founding households and a treasury of zero. By year's end, $8,400 in dues and a $3,200 neighborhood improvement grant had funded new benches, the east-side streetlights, and the first phase of the 5th Street Community Garden. Three years in, 43 households contribute monthly. Five projects are underway. And the vacant lot is becoming something.

Founding Charter
The Meridian District Collective
v1.2.0 · Ratified Jan 1, 2022 · 31 founding signatures · Amended twice
Article I — Purpose
This Collective exists to organize, fund, and govern community development in the Eastside Meridian District — through democratic decision-making, transparent finances, and collective action that city systems have been unable or unwilling to provide.
Article II — Membership & Dues
Any household within the Meridian District boundary may join. Monthly dues of $48/household are allocated across five community funds. No household may be denied membership for inability to pay — the Equity Provision allows dues reduction by block captain petition.
Article III — Democratic Projects
Any resident may propose a project. Proposals requiring over $500 from collective funds require a 7-day vote. Projects over $2,000 require a 14-day vote and 60% approval. The SAR Engine analyzes all proposals against available funds and prior commitments before votes open.
Article IV — The Council
Five Block Captains serve as the governing Council — one per geographic block. They are elected annually by their block's households. The Council may execute decisions up to $200 without a full vote. All larger actions require the full collective.
Article V — The Public Ledger
Every financial transaction is recorded in the immutable Public Ledger, visible to any resident at any time. No expenditure above $50 may occur without a ledger entry. The SAR Engine enforces this absolutely. Transparency is not optional — it is constitutional.
